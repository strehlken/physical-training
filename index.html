Programming Rules
Key Rules

Rest: 2 min for sets of 8, 3 min for sets of 5, 4+ min for sets of 3 on main lifts. Accessories: no more than 2 min rest.

Round squat and bench to nearest 5 lbs.

Recalculate 1RMs after week 3 using the final 3-rep (or final top set) weight: est max = weight × (1 + reps / 30).

Test pull-up 1RM at end of week 3, use that to anchor block 2 pull-up weights.

Deadlift: if any week feels like a grind, hold the weight and don't force the jump.

Analysis & Comparison

Summary Takeaway

The considered program is built for a lifter with more time (6 days/week), full gym access (dumbbells, machines, cables, dip bars), and no active injury constraints. Its core structural ideas are excellent — the 2×3-week block with mid-cycle recalculation, the high-rep / low-rep split for squat and bench, and the systematic volume management. Three of these ideas transfer directly to Nick's setup. The equipment-dependent accessories, Olympic lifts, and 6-day frequency do not. Bench and pull-ups, having graduated from rehab, can now be programmed as real lifts using similar structure — that's the biggest practical gain from studying this program.

Inferred Goals & Constraints of the Considered Program
What This Program Is Trying to Do

Primary goal: Squat 405, with bench and deadlift progressing in parallel. This is a strength-focused block with high training volume.

Structure: Descending sets (5→4→3) within each 3-week wave. Weight climbs as sets drop. Block 2 recalculates from block 1 results and shifts rep targets lower (8s→5s on "high rep" days, 5s→3s on "low rep" days). This is clean auto-regulation — total volume decreases naturally as intensity increases.

Frequency: 6 training days per week. Each major lift hit twice: squat Mon (8s) + Fri (5s/3s), bench Tue (8s) + Sat (5s/3s). Pull-ups Wed + Sat. Running Thu + Sat post-session.

Equipment: Full gym — barbell, dumbbells, machines (lat pulldown), dip bars. Allows substantial accessory volume: incline DB press, skull crushers, chest-supported rows, lat pulldowns, step-ups, dips.

Conditioning: Dedicated run day plus a post-session run on Saturday. Aerobic base is part of the plan.

Olympic work: Snatches used as movement primers 3×/week (from knee, hip, floor). Technical — not for load. Suggests the lifter has an Olympic lifting background or is developing one.

Injury status: None mentioned. No rehab protocols, no pain rules, no movement restrictions. Everything is programmed aggressively from day one.

Where This Differs From Nick's Constraints

Training Days cannot adopt

6 days vs 4. This is the single biggest gap. The considered program distributes work across 50% more training days. Compressing their volume into 4 sessions would blow past 45-minute caps or create unsustainable fatigue. Nick's schedule is fixed.

Equipment cannot adopt

DB press, skull crushers, chest-supported rows, lat pulldowns, step-ups, dips — none possible with Nick's setup. Nick's accessory pool is limited to barbell movements (RDLs, front squats, good mornings, barbell rows) and bodyweight (pull-ups, core). The volume those machine/DB accessories provide cannot be replicated 1:1, but the main lifts and barbell accessories cover the essential muscle groups.

Olympic Lifts does not apply

Snatches are a skill-specific movement that serves this lifter's development. Not relevant to Nick's goals and not worth introducing without coaching and the right equipment context.

No Injury Management cannot adopt

The considered program has no pain rules, no rehab tracks, no conditional movements. Nick has an active shoulder (governing bench and pull-up loading), a lower back on watch (RDLs conditional), and a right knee being monitored. Even as bench and pull-ups graduate from rehab protocol, the pain rule stays as a safety net for at least one more block. Rows remain trial-only.

What Transfers — And How

2 × 3-Week Block Structure with Mid-Cycle Recalculation adopted

This is the cleanest structural improvement available. Instead of projecting 6 weeks of numbers upfront (current approach), commit to a 3-week block, use the week 3 top sets to recalculate estimated maxes via formula (weight × (1 + reps/30)), and derive block 2 weights from real data. The skeleton stays the same — same days, same movements, same philosophy — but the numbers update based on performance. This eliminates the guesswork in weeks 4-6 and gives a built-in checkpoint. Deload follows as week 7.

High-Rep / Low-Rep Split for Squat adopted

Nick already squats twice per week, but both sessions use similar rep ranges (4-5 on heavy day, 6 on volume day). The considered program sharply differentiates: one day at 8s, one day at 5s/3s. For the 300-for-reps goal, this is a better split. Heavy day builds top-end strength with low-rep work. Volume day at 8s builds the muscular endurance and work capacity that lets you actually grind out a set of 5+ at 300 when the time comes. Adopted: heavy day stays as top set + backoffs at descending reps (5→4→3→4→3→2), volume day shifts from 6s to 8s at proportionally lighter weight.

Bench Programmed as a Real Lift, Twice Per Week adopted

With bench graduating from rehab, there's no reason it can't receive the same programming treatment as squat — a heavier day (Tue, lower reps) and a volume day (Sat, higher reps). The considered program runs bench this way with the same descending-sets wave. Nick's version uses the same days (Tue/Sat) with descending reps and ascending weight, structured within the 2×3-week block framework. Pain rule stays as a safety net but is no longer the primary programming driver.

Pull-Up Volume for Endurance Goal adapted

The considered program runs weighted pull-ups for strength. Nick's goal is 20 bodyweight reps — an endurance target. The structural idea of programming pull-ups 2×/week with real intent transfers, but the execution is different: bodyweight only, progressive rep accumulation, building toward sets of 8-10+ before eventually consolidating into fewer bigger sets. Weighted pull-ups become relevant later once 12-15 clean reps are established.

Conditioning / Running not adopted

The considered program includes a dedicated run day and a post-session run. Not adopted — Nick's 4-day schedule doesn't have room for a dedicated conditioning day, and adding runs after 45-minute sessions pushes past the time cap. Conditioning is outside the current scope.

Descending Sets Wave (5→4→3 sets) not adopted

The considered program's signature move — dropping from 5 working sets to 4 to 3 while weight climbs. This works when you have long sessions and can absorb 5 sets of heavy squats in week 1. Within 45 minutes and 3-4 movements per day, starting at 5 working sets would either blow session time or force cutting accessories that matter. Nick's existing approach of stable set counts with descending reps achieves the same fatigue-management intent in a tighter package. Left alone.

Programming Notes
Bench — Now a Real Lift

Bench graduates from rehab protocol to full programming. Heavy day (Tue) runs 5s→5s→4s in block 1, then 4s→3s→2s in block 2. Volume day (Sat) runs 8s in block 1, 6s→6s→5s in block 2. The pain rule stays as a safety net: 0-3/10 during set, baseline by next morning. If a weight doesn't pass the pain rule, drop 10 lbs and rebuild. But the pain rule is no longer the primary programming driver — intensity progression is.

Pull-ups — Endurance Path to 20

Goal is 20 bodyweight reps. This block focuses on volume accumulation at bodyweight only. Block 1: 3×6 → 3×7 → 4×6. Block 2: 4×7 → 4×8 → 4×8. The progression is reps per set first, then adding sets. No weight added this block. Weighted pull-ups become relevant later — once you can do 12-15 clean reps and want to build a higher strength ceiling to make 20 feel easier. That's 2-3 blocks away.

Volume Day at 8s

Shifted from 6s to 8s based on analysis. The 300-for-reps goal means you need to grind out 5+ reps at a heavy weight. Sets of 8 at moderate load build the muscular endurance and work capacity that makes that possible. Heavy day drives top-end strength with progressively lower reps. The two days now provide sharply different stimuli instead of overlapping in the 4-6 rep zone.

Mid-Cycle Recalculation

After week 3, use your top set results to estimate new 1RMs: est max = weight × (1 + reps / 30). Enter these in the Block 2 calculator above. Block 2 uses the same skeleton — same days, same movements — with weights derived from real performance data instead of upfront projections. This eliminates guesswork and lets you correct if you overshot or undershot.

Standing Rules

Equipment: barbell, squat rack, flat bench, pull-up bar. No dumbbells, machines, or cables.

RPE: top sets ~RPE 8. If something feels like a grinder, it's too heavy — drop 5 lbs.

Session time: ≤45 minutes. If running long, cut optional core first, then trim a backoff set.

Rows: still trial-only. Drop if shoulder objects.

RDLs: monitor lower back. If it speaks up, pull them immediately.

Knee: if the right knee ache persists from the previous block, reduce Sat volume load or ensure Mon (not Sun) for heavy squats to add a rest day after volume day.
